# How to Estimate Construction Costs - Digital Builder

Inaccurate estimates might not be the root of all evil, but they sure can derail a project. When projects go over budget, they result in frustration, delays, missed timelines, terminated relationships, and loss of reputation for companies.

[As Runn reports](https://www.runn.io/blog/why-do-projects-go-over-budget), “budget and time limitations are the biggest cause of stress for project leaders, towering above the challenges of change management, meeting high expectations, and responsibility for helping other team members develop their skills.”

Even with that said, the number of projects that fail to hit their budget targets is rather shocking. According to the above source, “35% of project failures were related to budget problems in 2021” and “only 43% of organizations completed most of their projects within budget during 2020.” Plus, “as many as 86% of projects are found to exceed their initial estimate, each construction project costing an average 28% more than anticipated.”

Blown budgets result from many reasons, from poor communication and missing data to scope creep to external factors like material price increases. Far and away one of the most common reasons, however, is inaccurate budget estimates. When it comes to construction, estimating costs is a towering challenge.

That’s why it’s so important to understand what construction cost estimation is and how to do it. Let’s dive in.

Construction cost [estimating](https://constructionblog.autodesk.com/construction-estimating/) systematically forecasts all costs associated with completing a project within a defined scope. In other words, *any* cost incurred to see a project through must fall under this heading.

The cost estimate serves as a roadmap for a project to be bid on and built. Accurate construction cost estimates help determine if a project is feasible to build within a budget.

If a project is overestimated, a contractor may lose out on an important bid and tarnish a relationship with an owner. Underestimate, and a contractor is now open to the risk of additional expenses that could create significant project disputes that put their reputation and business on the line.

Accuracy is key and where construction cost estimating 101 comes in.

First and foremost, an estimator needs to understand a project’s scope comprehensively. Therefore, they are responsible for determining the project scope and objectives from the beginning, including what the owner or client expects from the completed project, any future expansions (if planned), and how the facility will be used by the end user.

As a natural extension of this requirement, they must also identify key stakeholders upfront and determine the timelines needed to complete each phase of the project. The best way to ensure you capture all the details is to break down different tasks and deliverables into smaller tasks. This will make it easier to calculate costs and plan the project.

The next step in estimating construction costs is to gather the information and documentation pertaining to the project. From there, you can analyze project designs, drawings, and specifications, then identify potential risks and challenges.

To do this, be sure to analyze all of the construction documents and factors like construction methods, materials, and more.

A [construction takeoff](https://constructionblog.autodesk.com/construction-takeoff/) is the “process of listing and measuring the materials required for the project to calculate the cost to complete it. The construction takeoff is the first step in the estimation process and involves quantifying the materials necessary to start and execute the job.”

Remember that in addition to listing the materials needed for the project, you need to know their respective quantities and costs too.

Construction cost estimates fall into two basic categories, and even the most hardened estimator is prone to miss details if they’re not careful. Let’s take a look at some of the common types of costs, then discuss tools and methods for estimating what the construction estimating costs would be for the project.

Direct costs include labor, materials determined from the construction takeoff, equipment, and subcontractor costs.

It’s easy to forget about indirect costs when estimating construction costs. However, the cost of a project will include jobsite and trailer costs, admin costs, office personnel, insurance, taxes, permits, and personal protective and safety equipment.
